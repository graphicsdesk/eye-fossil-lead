Columbia has received funding from fossil fuel companies for decades. That money keeps flowing.

By Jesse Levine and Itzel Franco

October 9, 2023

Illustration by Kelsea Petersen

In 2020, Columbia established the Climate School, a “first of its kind” institution that would grant master’s degrees to the next generation of climate activists, policymakers, and leaders. The following year, Columbia adopted a formal fossil fuel investment policy, announcing at the time that the University “does not hold any direct investment in publicly traded oil and gas companies, and is formalizing this policy of non-investment for the foreseeable future.”

Climate Week NYC 2023, “the largest annual climate event of its kind, bringing together some 400 events and activities across the City of New York,” according to its website, chose The Climate School as its university partner.

But at least three projects at the Climate School have been funded by major fossil fuel companies, The Eye found. And in the 2021 fiscal year, when the University’s board of trustees adopted its fossil fuel investment policy, the School of International and Public Affairs received donations of more than $1 million from Occidental Petroleum Corporation and between $100,000 and $499,999 each from BP, Cheniere Energy, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, ExxonMobil, and Shell, according to the SIPA website.

Based on archival records of Exxon’s research with Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory scientists in the late 1970s and early 1980s, fossil fuel company gift reports, and interviews with former industry employees, The Eye found a history of fossil fuel companies’ involvement with Columbia that continues today. The University has spent much of the last 50 years with a range of direct and indirect connections to the fossil fuel industry, which has grown increasingly influential in academia’s relationship to the climate.

According to researchers’ personal archives, at least 12 projects conducted by Columbia researchers from 1987 to 1993 were funded by Exxon. Today, fossil fuel influence continues to spill onto campus through continued research funding across the University and through the presence of fossil fuel company officials on the advisory board of the Center on Global Energy Policy, a SIPA-based center that “develops evidence-based research to help address the world’s most challenging energy and climate problems through research, education, and dialogue.”

The Early Days

In 1973, after Spectator repeatedly requested that then-University President William McGill release a list of Columbia’s bond and stock holdings, McGill released a portfolio showing that Columbia held over $1 million of stock in Mobil Oil, Standard Oil of Indiana, Standard Oil of New Jersey—the predecessor of Exxon, which in 1999 merged with Mobil to become ExxonMobil—Phillips Petroleum, and Texaco, each.

Later that year, McGill was named to Texaco’s board of directors. The following year, Barnard’s president at the time, Martha Peterson, was named to Exxon’s board of directors.

In a September interview with The Eye, professor emeritus of history Robert McCaughey, author of Stand, Columbia, a comprehensive history of the University, said it was possible for others to see McGill’s public involvement “as a positive activity on the part of Columbia.” As for student pushback, especially in the years after the 1968 protests, McCaughey noted fossil fuel advocacy was a “niche issue.”

McGill spoke openly about his new position at Texaco, Spectator reported in September 1973. McGill said he had not consulted with Columbia’s board of trustees before accepting the corporate role and acknowledged that his new position might be politically controversial.

Almost exactly three months after McGill’s announcement, the Barnard-Columbia Attica Brigade, a student-run subsection of the national Revolutionary Union—which later became the Revolutionary Communist Party—called for McGill’s resignation from the Texaco board in a Spectator op-ed. The group also demanded that Columbia “divest itself of its $1.7 million in Texaco stock.”

The Barnard-Columbia Attica Brigade’s primary concern was Texaco’s role in the “oppression and exploitation of people all over,” the letter read. The brigade did not appear to be concerned about environmental issues, and the term “climate change” had yet to be popularized.

In April 1978, a member of the Revolutionary Student Brigade, the Attica Brigade’s successor, led a small group of students protesting for South African divestment across Low Steps after McGill attended a Columbia board of trustees meeting. The discussions were restricted to an account of McGill’s conversations with Black South African leaders and were not focused on divestment, Spectator reported. The students were particularly upset with McGill’s role at Texaco, which was known for its exploitative practices in South Africa.

In the late 1950s and early ’60s, Columbia was heavily reliant on federal contracts to support science research—like the kind happening at Lamont, McCaughey said. But in the mid 1960s, as federal funding was reallocated to support the Vietnam War, McGill told Spectator, “it was clear that I must turn my attention more forcefully than ever to fund-raising.”

Peterson called her Exxon board position “a nice opportunity to make some money for Barnard.”

In October 1975, Columbia received $500,000 in donations to renovate Havemeyer Hall, including “two $100,000 gifts from the Exxon Foundation Fund and the DuPont Foundation,” Spectator reported at the time. Adjusting for inflation, one $100,000 gift is equivalent to about $570,000 today.

The Esso Atlantic

In the 1970s and 1980s, Exxon was at the cutting edge of climate research. Central to this research was a project known as the Esso Atlantic—an oil tanker outfitted with carbon dioxide monitoring equipment—which brought together engineers and researchers from Exxon and scientists from Columbia.

A 2015 reporting series by the nonprofit newsroom Inside Climate News investigating what Exxon knew about the effects of its business practices on the environment called the Esso Atlantic the “crown jewel” of the company’s research program.

Wallace Broecker and Taro Takahashi were researchers at Lamont who consulted on the Esso Atlantic project in 1979. In June 1963, Broecker and Takahashi found themselves working together in an expedition to the Bahama Banks, according to an article Broecker published in 2008.

“There, first using a chartered Chalk Airline Widgeon and then a Lerner Lab research vessel, I became an oceanographer,” Broecker recalled in the article. Broecker and Takahashi died in 2019.

In December 1978, Henry Shaw, manager of the environmental area in Exxon research and engineering’s Technology Feasibility Center, wrote a letter to Edward E. David, then-president of Exxon research and engineering, in which he laid out his rationale behind Exxon’s involvement in the climate research area: to assess the possible impact of the greenhouse effect on Exxon business. Shaw wrote that he was looking to establish a “credible scientific team,” singling out Lamont to begin a “cooperative program” based on the oceanography expertise of Broecker and Takahashi.

That spring, Exxon representatives recruited Edward Garvey, a chemical engineering major at The Cooper Union, to work on what he said was described as a “new, exciting environmental investigation program.”

“[Exxon] wanted to be involved in how society would change its fossil fuel mixture, how fossil fuels would be regulated,” Garvey said in an interview with The Eye. “They wanted to have real scientists that were respected in the industry to say, ‘These are the concerns.’”

Garvey said Takahashi and Broecker, whom he saw as mentors, “only agreed to be consultants [for Exxon] if they had free access to the data and no limitations on whatever they wanted to say anytime, anywhere, any place.”

Garvey said Broecker was initially skeptical about Exxon funding.

“Exxon had to convince Wally that we really were serious about it. Exxon, Wally saw them potentially just as industrial hacks just trying to get something done,” Garvey said.

Garvey would work on the project for five years. During that time, in September 1979, he enrolled at Columbia to get his doctorate in geochemistry with the help of Exxon’s employee tuition reimbursement program.

The 1980s Oil Glut

Then, in 1982, Exxon shut down the Esso Atlantic.

Exxon saw strong revenues in the 1970s amid high global oil prices. But a global recession in the 1980s, combined with the nations of the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries asserting control over oil production, brought Exxon’s production of crude oil from 6.8 million barrels per day in 1973 to about 1.7 million in 1985, according to Joseph A. Pratt’s “Exxon and the Control of Oil.”

In June 1982, A. M. Natkin, the Exxon science and technology department’s environmental affairs coordinator, sent a letter to top Exxon research official Harold Weinberg informing him that the company’s climate research program budget would be cut from $900,000 to $385,000 that year. The funding was then slashed further to $150,000 in 1983.

“The tanker study was seen [by Exxon] as a lot of money for no net profit, nothing other than PR,” Garvey said.

Garvey left Exxon in 1983 amid company layoffs. After switching his course of study from chemical oceanography to estuary studies—he didn’t like being at sea for long periods of time—Garvey completed his doctorate in February 1990. He now teaches the course Environmental Investigation and Remediation at the School of Professional Studies.

Still, Exxon funding continued to trickle into Columbia’s records. In over a dozen “VITA Binders” stored in an unprocessed collection at Columbia’s Rare Book and Manuscript Library, Broecker kept a chronological record of his publications spanning over his six decades as a researcher. The binders include Broecker’s work at Lamont, as well as his contributions to various scientific magazines and journals.

Exxon funded a total of 12 projects authored by either Takahashi or Broecker from 1987 to 1993, The Eye found through a review of the VITA Binders and Taro Takahashi’s personal records.

‘Numerous and productive relationships’

Hadeel Assali worked at ExxonMobil for nearly 10 years, joining the company in 2002 as a chemical engineer and then transitioning into its environmental remediation division in 2008. She left Exxon in 2011 and went on to earn her doctorate in anthropology from Columbia in 2021. Now, she is a postdoctoral research scholar and lecturer in earth and environmental sciences at the University.

Assali described the main goal of Exxon’s “environmental remediation team” as “bounding liability.” Assali said that while working in environmental remediation, she was not allowed to use the word “contamination” in reference to an oil spill, and that the “public affairs” team would read reports with a fine-tooth comb.

“There were certain things that we just wouldn’t—like you just wouldn’t release the report right away until there was a way to massage it or deal with it in a certain way,” Assali said.

Exxon did not respond to repeated requests for comment.

In 2015, a team of journalists working on the Energy and Environmental Reporting Project at the Journalism School provided a glimpse into Exxon’s relationship with Columbia.

The team reviewed internal company documents housed in archives in the Glenbow Museum and at the University of Texas. The team published a series of three articles with the Los Angeles Times asserting that Exxon company officials had been aware of climate change risks since the 1980s.

Kenneth P. Cohen, then ExxonMobil’s senior vice president for public and government affairs, responded to the reporting in a November 2015 letter to former University President Lee Bollinger that noted the “numerous and productive” relationships Exxon has had with Columbia. Politico reported that ExxonMobil had given Columbia “more than $200,000” in 2014.

In the letter, Cohen wrote that the reporters had not given ExxonMobil sufficient time to comment and that one of the journalists had not accurately identified herself.

“The pursuit of this false narrative is particularly disturbing given that ExxonMobil has a continuous history of researching climate science that stretches back nearly 40 years,” Cohen wrote, citing the company’s research work in conjunction with the United Nations, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, and Columbia itself.

“The interactions detailed above are not typical of the high standards and ethical behavior we have come to expect from your institution,” Cohen wrote.

Steve Coll, dean of the Journalism School at the time, responded to Cohen in a December 2015 letter addressing each of Cohen’s charges.

“I have concluded that your allegations are unsupported by evidence,” Coll wrote. “More than that, I have been troubled to discover that you have made serious allegations of professional misconduct in your letter against members of the project team even though you or your Media Relations colleagues possess email records showing that your allegations are false.”

In December 2022, the U.S. House of Representatives Committee on Oversight and Reform released hundreds of internal corporate documents from major fossil fuel companies obtained by the committee as part of a probe “into the fossil fuel industry’s long-running campaign to mislead the American people about fossil fuels’ central role in causing the global climate crisis and to impede urgently needed efforts to curb climate change.”

ExxonMobil “was given approximately a month” to respond and “the company knew” the reporter “had represented herself accurately,” according to the committee’s memorandum, which cited internal ExxonMobil emails.

Following the exchange of complaints between ExxonMobil and the Journalism School in 2015, eight climate advocacy groups including Public Citizen, Columbia Divest for Climate Justice, and the Checks and Balances Project sent a letter to Bollinger calling on the University to reveal the funders of its Center on Global Energy Policy, which opened in 2013.

Columbia’s “Investment Policy on Fossil Fuels”

Bollinger established the Climate School in July 2020 as the “nation’s first climate school” and Columbia’s first new school in 25 years. It opened to its first class of master’s in climate and society students in August 2021.

The climate and society master’s program, which used to be housed at the Earth Institute at Columbia, began in 2004.

Then, in January 2021, the Columbia board of trustees approved the University's current fossil fuel investment stance. A Columbia spokesperson did not respond to a question about the breakdown of the vote.

The University’s investment policy on fossil fuels, which is still in place, “formalizes investment practices related to the oil and gas industry, defined for this purpose as companies whose primary business is the exploration and production of fossil fuels, or integrated oil and gas companies whose business includes the exploration, production and refining and marketing of oil and gas.”

The policy included a commitment that Columbia would not make any “new investments in private funds that primarily invest in oil and gas companies.”

“There is an undeniable obligation binding upon Columbia and other universities to confront the climate crisis across every dimension of our institutions,” Bollinger said in a January 2021 news release.

Matthew Ruppert, a Columbia College senior and president of the Columbia Policy Institute, Columbia’s undergraduate policy think tank, criticized the policy for its failure to numerically specify the meaning of the word “primarily” in Columbia’s investment portfolio numbers.

Anika Kathuria, a junior in Columbia College and president of Columbia’s chapter of Sunrise, a student-led climate advocacy organization, said that Sunrise has been working in tandem with CPI to ask the administration for a “more transparent divestment policy.”

The University spokesperson did not clarify the policy’s definition of “primarily.”

Despite the investment policy, ExxonMobil, Occidental Petroleum Corporation, BP, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, and Shell have continued to funnel dollars into campus through donations and research funding. ExxonMobil, Occidental, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, and Shell did not respond to requests for comment.

A BP spokesperson pointed The Eye to its online policy on university collaboration. The website lists partnerships with universities in the United States including Princeton University, Tufts University, and Harvard University.

Columbia received at least $5,509,450 from leading fossil fuel companies including ExxonMobil between the years of 2010 and 2020, according to a study by Data for Progress, a progressive think tank and polling firm.

A Columbia spokesperson did not comment on the study.

Bella Kumar, a junior at George Washington University who authored the study, said most of the dollar amounts in the report were sourced from publically-available charitable donation forms the fossil fuel companies submitted to the IRS.

“It’s kind of funny that the fossil fuel industry isn’t really trying to cover their tracks,” Kumar said. “It’s the universities.”

Columbia does not disclose specific donation amounts, nor do many other universities. A Columbia spokesperson did comment on the University’s policy on accepting or disclosing fossil fuel donations.

Out of the 27 universities that leading fossil fuel companies donated and pledged money to from 2010 to 2020 that were included in the Data for Progress study, Columbia ranked 16th, receiving at least $5,509,450. Princeton University ranked seventh, receiving $36,096,163, and Harvard University ranked 10th, receiving $21,142,825, according to the study.

Columbia’s policies surrounding conflicts of interest in research are outlined on the website of Columbia’s Office of the Executive Vice President for Research.

“The University’s policies addressing conflicts of interest in research are designed to maintain the trust of the public, research volunteers, and the University research community and to protect against risks to research integrity, research participants and the academic mission that may result from individual or institutional conflicts of interest in research,” the website reads.

The Eye has found three past projects at the Climate School that have received funding from fossil fuel companies. ExxonMobil, a subsidiary of Royal Dutch Shell, and Chevron have each sponsored one.

The project sponsored by ExxonMobil, titled “Investigating Drivers of Particulate Matter Pollution over India and the Implications for Climate” was active from January 2019 to June 2021, according to the Climate School website.

Alexandra Karambelas, a former researcher at Columbia who worked on that project, said the ExxonMobil collaborator with whom she worked would have to submit abstracts or the article to higher-ups in the research and engineering department to ensure that the research was “good” before the team of researchers published the results of their investigation. Karambelas said that she did not know what “good” meant, but said the process was not out of the ordinary and that she had undergone a similar process when receiving state funding for projects.

The other two publicly listed principal investigators on the project, Arlene Fiore and Daniel Westervelt, did not respond to repeated requests for comment. Exxon did not respond to repeated requests for comment.

Kerry Key is an associate professor in the department of earth and environmental sciences and the lead principal investigator on “Electromagnetic Methods Research Consortium: Shell International Exploration and Production Inc. Sponsorship,” sponsored by Shell from January 2019 to December 2020, and Electromagnetic Methods Research Consortium: Chevron Sponsorship, sponsored by Chevron Corporation from January 2018 to December 2020 for a value of $50,000, according to the Climate School website In a statement to The Eye, he wrote that the research uses computational modeling codes to map economic petroleum reserves.

“It’s basic geophysical research and has nothing to do with policy or climate science,” Key wrote.

Shell and Chevron did not respond to repeated requests for comment.

“We take seriously the integrity of Columbia research and have robust conflict of interest policies in place to ensure that donors and funders are not able to dictate or influence any research or its outcomes,” a Columbia spokesperson wrote to The Eye. “These policies are guided by the University’s sacrosanct values when it comes to academic freedom and maintaining the public’s trust.”

Page Fortna, the Harold Brown professor of U.S. foreign and security policy in the department of political science, is the new director of the Arnold A. Saltzman Institute of War and Peace Studies and contributes to planning its approach to fundraising. Though she didn’t specifically comment on the Climate School’s fundraising strategy, she said it is often difficult to balance taking funding opportunities when they come with the complicated ethical backgrounds of the companies behind some donations and their reasons for giving.

“There’s a certain amount of, ‘Take money from wherever you can get it to do good with it,’” Fortna said in an interview with The Eye. “On the other hand, I think it is enabling companies to make themselves look good.”

“Columbia doesn’t have a policy against taking money from fossil fuel interests. I personally think that it should, but it doesn’t, or it doesn’t yet,” she added.

ExxonMobil’s website lists the company’s four decades of climate science research resulting in over 50 peer-reviewed publications, emphasizing its work funding climate modeling at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology and the development of “lower-emission solutions” at Stanford University.

“The moment [fossil fuel companies are] able to say that something that they’ve done has been in collaboration with a prestigious university, people’s defenses go down,” Jake Lowe, the executive director of Fossil Free Research, an organization of students and faculty working to expose and dismantle fossil fuel influence on higher education, said. “They’re far less likely to be skeptical, or think about all the ways where there can still be industry bias or disinformation or all of those things.”

Fossil Free Research has issued an open letter to university presidents and vice-chancellors, calling on U.K. and U.S. universities to institute a ban on accepting fossil fuel industry funding for climate change, environmental, and energy policy research. Included among the over 950 signatories were four professors at Columbia: A. A. Roell, Frederica Perera, Alyssa Battistoni, and Douglas Almond.

“Universities banning categories of donors has precedent. The damage from continued high use of fossil fuels exceeds that from tobacco. Both industries lied to the public, and many top universities prohibit tobacco donations,” Almond, an economics professor, co-director for the Center for Environmental Economics and Policy, and the vice dean of academic affairs at SIPA, wrote in a statement to The Eye.

In an interview with The Eye, Battistoni said she “agree[s] with the letter pretty wholeheartedly.”

In reference to the letter, Perera, professor of environmental health sciences and founder of the Columbia Center for Children's Environmental Health, said in an interview with The Eye that she “really felt that it’s the same story as for tobacco—that the best course is not to accept funding from these companies.”

Roell did not respond to requests for comment.

The Data for Progress study also included a poll of 1,268 likely voters which found that the majority agree with the statement: “colleges and universities studying the impacts of climate change and sustainability should refuse donations from fossil fuel companies so they can remain unbiased in their research.” Part of the study informed participants that Columbia has used donations from the fossil fuel industry to fund climate and environmental research, and follow-up polling found that Columbia’s favorability dropped from 48 percent to 42 percent.

“I knew a lot of this would come down to public pressure,” Kumar said. “The University is [a] business, right? They’re concerned with their image, they’re concerned with PR, they’re concerned with what’s going to make them money.”

Oil Stained Climate Research

In November 2022, a team of Columbia affiliates led by Almond published an article in Nature pointing to the substantive effect of fossil fuel funding on research produced by energy centers like the Center for Global Energy Policy.

“We were wondering whether there’s anything to test empirically regarding these ideas of what does industry funding do? Does it bias research in the climate and energy center space specifically?” Anna Papp, a doctoral student at Columbia who worked with Almond on the study, said in an interview with The Eye.

Almond’s team compiled research reports from energy centers at 26 universities and utilized an analysis tool that looked at the sentiment of each sentence of the report. The tool scored each sentence on a scale of negative one to positive one based on how negative or positive the sentence was. The researchers then compared the sentiment scores of sentences that have keywords related to natural gas to the average sentiment of all of the other sentences in the same report.

“We found that fossil-funded centres are more favourable in their reports towards natural gas than towards renewable energy, and tweets are more favourable when they mention funders by name,” the report read. “Centres less dependent on fossil funding show a reversed pattern with more neutral sentiment towards gas, and favour solar and hydro power.”

“Unlike some other prominent energy centers (cf. the University of Chicago’s EPIC), Columbia energy center reports do not typically face peer review by independent, scholarly journals,” Almond wrote in a statement to The Eye. “This removes a natural check on any pro-funder slant.”

A Columbia spokesperson wrote that CGEP reports do undergo an internal review, continuing that when submitted to outside academic journals, those reports also undergo the journal’s review process.

“We are surprised that the magnitude of positive sentiment towards natural gas in reports from fossil-funded energy centers like Columbia’s was so large,” Almond wrote. “Indeed, it was as positive as that coming from the fossil industry associations, whose explicit mission is to promote the fossil industry.”

One piece of research the team analyzed was a September 2014 CGEP report titled “American Gas to the Rescue? The Impact of US LNG Exports on European Security and Russian Foreign Policy,” authored by Jason Bordoff and Trevor Houser. The report found that U.S. exports would create a larger, more liquid global gas market, increasing supply options for gas consumers abroad. The report did not identify its funders.

Bordoff is the founding director of CGEP and the co-founding dean of the Climate School. Houser is a partner at the energy and climate practice at Rhodium Group, an independent research provider. CGEP and the Rhodium Group did not respond to requests for comment.

Almond said the CGEP report received significant press attention and was at times referred to as “the Columbia University report” without mention of funding. Richard Shelby, a U.S. senator for Alabama, cited the study in his opening remarks during a full committee hearing on “Lifting the Crude Oil Export Ban” in July 2015.

“Studies from Columbia University, the Brookings Institution, and the Government Accountability Office, among others cite that lifting the ban is likely to reduce the price of gasoline for American consumers by increasing the supply of crude oil available to the world market,” Shelby said.

Congress lifted the 40-year oil export ban in December 2015, allowing the free export of U.S. crude oil worldwide. The U.S. exported fewer than half a million barrels per day in 2015, according to a study by the U.S. Government Accountability Office. By 2019, that number had grown to almost 3 million barrels per day.

CGEP’s advisory board includes Charif Souki and Martin Houston, the executive chairman and vice chairman of Tellurian Inc., a natural gas company responsible for producing roughly 79 percent of U.S. liquified natural gas, according to the company’s website. Tellurian supports the Charif Souki Global Energy Fellows program at CGEP, according to the center’s website.

CGEP lists two companies under its “Visionary Annual Circle,” its highest corporate partnership tier composed of partners with annual or multi-year aggregate commitments to SIPA of $1 million or more.

Those companies are Tellurian Inc. and Occidental Petroleum Corporation.

***

Last spring, the Energy and Environment Center at CPI authored a letter to the administrations of incoming University President Minouche Shafik and Barnard President Laura Rosenbury in which they criticized the Bollinger administration for being “often slow, unyielding, opaque, and dismissive of student concerns.”

The letter covered several environmental issues on campus, including divestment from fossil fuels, fossil free research, energy sourcing at Columbia, building efficiency, and water use.

In December 2022, Ruppert and CPI called on the University’s advisory committee on socially responsible investing to recommend that the trustees direct the Columbia Investment Management Company, which manages the “bulk” of the University’s $13.28 billion endowment, to “cease all remaining, and abstain from any future investments in, private funds which are invested in public and private fossil fuel companies.” ACSRI, established in 2000, advises the trustees “on ethical and social issues that arise in the management of the investments in the University’s endowment.”

The response, written by ACSRI faculty chair Bruce Usher, includes a clarification from the Columbia Investment Management Company on its “position on investments in funds with exposure to fossil fuel companies.” The IMC specified that through generalist co-mingled funds, 0.73 percent of Columbia’s overall portfolio is indirectly exposed to “energy investments.” The IMC further established that 0.31 percent of Columbia’s total portfolio was exposed through exchange-traded funds, as of the end of the 2022 calendar year.

“It is the opinion of the ACSRI that the IMC should continue to have the flexibility to invest broadly in funds and [exchange-traded funds] that do not have a strategy of primarily investing in fossil fuel companies,” Usher wrote.

Ruppert said ACSRI’s response “sort of seemed to minimize any of the concerns that [CPI] raised.” The response came on May 17—the day of 2023 commencement—so Ruppert said the group had little time to take action.

Kathuria emphasized the important of awareness—that members of the Columbia administration hold ties to the fossil fuel industry, that the divestment policy has gaps, and that companies that recruit Columbia students contribute to the fossil fuel ecosystem— but acknowledged that it’s “not an individual student’s responsibility to “figure this out.”

Ruppert said that CPI met with Senior Executive Vice President Gerald M. Rosberg over the summer and discussed, among other things, how students can communicate with the University and vice versa.

Rosberg did not respond to a request for comment.
